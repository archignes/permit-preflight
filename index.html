Seattle Mobile Food Vendor Permit Preflight

We asked five AI models how to start a taco truck in Seattle. They all gave confident answers. They all missed the same thing: where you park determines which office you contact first, and getting that wrong can cost months. This page shows the problem, shows what curiosity-driven questioning finds instead, and prepares a better handoff to the real human offices.

Do not use this to decide whether you can operate. This page has not been reviewed or approved by Seattle, King County, or any permitting office. It is a draft for review by city planners and the food truck community.

Part 1The test prompt

We gave five frontier AI models the same plain question—no system prompt, no extra context—and compared what they said to what the city's own sources actually require. The question:

"I want to run a taco truck in Seattle near breweries and sometimes at neighborhood events. What do I need before I contact the city?"

This is a realistic first ask. It sounds simple, but it hides a routing problem: where the truck parks determines which office comes first, and every model we tested missed that fork.

Part 1 / IntakeRecognition before methodology

The prompt hides the jurisdictional fork.

"Near breweries" sounds simple, but it could mean a brewery parking lot, the adjacent public curb, a city park, or a neighborhood event footprint. A useful preflight surface should slow down here, not generate a checklist.

The moment of recognition: a vendor can have one permit and still be missing the permit that matters at that site.

Seattle public radio station KUOW's July 29, 2024 story, "A Seattle hot dog with everything—but not all the permits," is the best narrative evidence: a vendor had county health permission and handwashing equipment but lacked a Parks permit.

That is the civic-AI failure this page is designed to surface: not "unpermitted versus permitted," but wrong-route confidence.

Part 3 / Routing discoveriesThe curious move

Six discoveries before advice.

The first useful output is not a paragraph. It is a set of hidden-structure discoveries that make the applicant say, "I did not know that mattered."

1. The phrase "near breweries" hides the fork that changes everything.

Where exactly will you park? A public curb, sidewalk, plaza, private parking lot, city park, special event venue, or a mix?

Why this matters: public right-of-way, private property, parks, and events route through different offices. Seattle OED (Office of Economic Development) SDOT

2. The brewery lot and the curb beside it may require different city offices.

Do you have owner permission? Is the brewery's parking area private land, adjacent city right-of-way, or part of an event footprint? On private property, SDCI says no construction or use permit is needed only if: non-residential zone, owner permission, no overnight stay, parking-area location, and at least 50 feet from residentially zoned property. If any condition is not met, ask SDCI about temporary or permanent use permitting.

Why this matters: SDCI private-property conditions, SDOT curb use, and event organizer requirements are not interchangeable. Many compliant private-property cases need no SDCI permit at all — but you must confirm all five conditions first. SDCI

3. Fire is not a footnote; the cooking method can create a separate route.

Propane, LPG, charcoal, wood, solid fuel, open flame, electric, or deep frying?

Why this matters: Seattle Fire is the fire/fuel regulator, not the food regulator or the land-use regulator. SFD

4. "Truck" can hide a state approval dependency.

Occupied truck, occupied trailer, unenclosed cart, or kiosk?

Why this matters: occupied trucks and trailers may need Washington State L&I approval before King County final health approval. WA L&I

5. The commissary is not a later checklist item; it can block the first filing.

A commissary is a permitted food establishment where a food truck stores, preps, cleans, and disposes of wastewater between shifts—King County requires proof of one (or an exemption) before plan review can proceed. Do you have an approved commissary agreement? A restroom agreement within 500 feet for regular operation?

Why this matters: King County mobile food plan review requires commissary and restroom materials early. King County guide

6. "Near breweries" hides a liquor-premises question.

Is your truck inside or adjacent to a liquor-licensed premises? Is alcohol being served or catered at the location? Who controls the licensed boundary?

Why this matters: brewery-hosted operations can create confusion about who has permission to do what on the premises. Washington State Liquor and Cannabis Board (WSLCB) has restrictions on mobile food operations near or within liquor-licensed boundaries, even if the truck is not selling alcohol.
Commissary pricing ranges from $200/month (minimal cart use, limited hours) to $2,548/month (full commercial kitchen). For a working taco truck doing regular shifts, expect $750-$2,000+/month once parking, water, wastewater disposal, and usable access hours are included.
KUOW's July 2024 hot dog story: county health permit and handwashing equipment, but no Parks permit.
Reddit community voices describe wrong-office loops, "months and months," "$1500 a month," and guidance too messy to finish in a comment box.

Part 4B / Tentative routingSoft Ground markers

A tentative route, with uncertainty visible.

The page distinguishes assertions, conditionals, limitations, and recommendations. That makes reliance contestable instead of hidden inside confident prose.

limitationhigh uncertainty

I cannot determine the first city office from "near breweries."

The phrase could mean private brewery property, adjacent public curb space, city park activity, or an event footprint. That site fact decides the route.

conditionalmedium uncertainty

If the brewery site is private property, start with owner permission and SDCI conditions.

SDCI says private-property vending may avoid a construction or use permit only under conditions including non-residential zone, owner permission, no overnight stay, parking-area location, and distance from residential zoning.

assertionlow uncertainty

If the spot is public curb, sidewalk, or plaza, SDOT is the Street Use route.

OED and SDOT sources both route public right-of-way vending to SDOT Street Use vending permits.

assertionlow uncertainty

Food safety routes through Public Health - Seattle & King County.

The mobile food unit guide names plan review, commissary agreement, restroom agreement, menu, site information, operating permit, and inspection materials.

conditionalmedium uncertainty

If the truck uses propane, charcoal, solid fuel, open flame, or oil frying equipment, include Seattle Fire.

If the unit is fully electric, the fire route may be different and should be confirmed before telling the applicant SFD applies.

recommendationhuman handoff

Stop when site type, event footprint, fuel, unit type, or health readiness is unknown.

The system's job is to prepare a better question for the right office, not to decide permit sufficiency.

Preflight pathway sketch draft - known incomplete

  1. State-level first: WA State business license (UBI number) and sales tax permit (WA Dept. of Revenue). These often come before city filings.
  2. Sort each location: public right-of-way, private property, park, event, or mixed site.
  3. Match location office: SDOT, SDCI no-permit conditions, Parks, Special Events, event organizer, or another office.
  4. Confirm health readiness: King County plan review, commissary, restroom, menu, inspection, or exemption.
  5. Check unit sequence: L&I for occupied truck/trailer before final King County approval when applicable.
  6. Check fire/fuel route: propane, LPG, charcoal, solid fuel, open flame, oil frying, or electric.
  7. Check liquor-premises context: if operating at or near a brewery or liquor-licensed location, confirm WSLCB requirements.
  8. Confirm business/event admin: Seattle business license, food worker cards, insurance, event organizer requirements, and time-limited event constraints.
  9. Stop for human review: exact address, dates, hours, unit type, fuel, menu, owner/event contact, and current permit status.

How AI fails on permitsPatterns worth testing

The failures become test cases.

These are the patterns of failure we found across all five models. Each one could be used to test whether a future AI system handles permits better. Inspired by Dave Guarino's work evaluating AI on public benefits questions.

Failure Mode Test Input Expected Bad Output What Makes It Bad Detection Signal
Overconfidence "What do I need?" "Get these permits and you're ready." Skips routing questions and implies a completable sequence. No conditional language; no "it depends"; no stop point.
Wrong jurisdiction "I need a food truck permit." Routes to one city office. Collapses county health, city location, fire, and state L&I responsibilities. Single agency named for a multi-agency stack.
Missing agency "I use propane near a brewery." Lists health permit and business license only. Omits the fire/fuel trigger. No mention of Seattle Fire, LPG, propane, open flame, solid fuel, or inspection.
Wrong sequence "What do I file first?" "Start with your business license." Business licensing does not determine the routing fork. Location type, health readiness, unit type, and event footprint are not asked first.
Staleness "What are the fees?" Cites a 2023 fee-waiver-era schedule as current. Fee waiver expired Jan. 1, 2026 and fees changed; FIFA and enforcement context shifted. No last-verified date and no caveat that fees and enforcement posture change.
